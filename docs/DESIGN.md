# Reach for the Sky — Game Design

This document owns game identity, player fantasy, and the complete player journey. System specifics live in [SYSTEMS.md](./SYSTEMS.md). Remaining production work lives in [PRODUCTION.md](./PRODUCTION.md).

## Identity

Reach for the Sky is a modern living-tower simulator where architecture, people, reputation, and city pressure collide.

It is not a nostalgia port, not a passive builder, and not a decorative skyline toy. The 2D cutaway is the primary interaction model because it is the clearest surface for understanding a living tower under pressure.

## Core Pillars

- Architecture matters: placement and adjacency change what the tower is and how it works.
- People matter: workers, guests, cohorts, inspectors, and visitors create pressure through behavior, not scripts.
- Reputation matters: the city remembers outcomes and reacts to what the tower becomes.
- Pressure matters: queues, dirt, privacy, safety, weather, noise, and service failures must create visible consequences.
- Identity matters: the tower should earn a role in the district, not just accumulate floors.

## Core Loop

1. Build spaces, services, and circulation.
2. Observe tenant and visitor behavior.
3. Diagnose emerging pressure through lenses, reports, and inspections.
4. Respond with layout, staffing, service, and hosting decisions.
5. Progress into new acts, contracts, and public expectations.
6. Repeat as the tower becomes more visible and politically exposed.

## Macro / Meso / Micro

- Macro: district identity, demand, regulation, weather risk, fame, trust, and skyline status.
- Meso: floor bands, transit topology, service coverage, venue credibility, safety readiness, privacy, noise, and operations grade.
- Micro: routed agents and cohorts with traits, moods, dealbreakers, memories, and Yuka-driven planning.

The design requirement is that all three layers feed each other. Macro creates opportunity and scrutiny. Meso determines whether the tower can handle it. Micro creates the stories that the city remembers.

## Player Journey

### Act 1 — Empty Lot

Teach the build grammar:

- lobby
- floors
- offices
- elevator core
- first rent loop
- first bottleneck recognition

### Act 2 — Working Tower

Teach operations:

- dirt and service pressure
- daily reports
- sentiment and rent leakage
- basic reactive contracts

### Act 3 — District Player

Force a meaningful identity declaration:

- business hub
- vertical village
- event destination
- civic tower
- prestige tower
- mixed ecosystem

### Act 4 — Public Landmark

The tower becomes a public-stage object:

- visit cohorts
- public memory
- inspectors and press
- repair contracts
- scrutiny under failure

### Act 5 — Reach for the Sky

The tower becomes an institution:

- skyline charter
- city-cycle mandates
- sandbox continuation
- weather and public legitimacy pressure

## Differentiators

- No rote “VIP arrives at level 3” scripting.
- Cohorts emerge from current tower identity and conditions.
- Public memory produces contracts and recovery arcs.
- The tower is authored visually in 2D rather than abstracted into colored blocks.
- The game remains readable first, cinematic second.

## What The Game Should Feel Like

- In the first 10 minutes: lucid, tactile, and operationally legible.
- In the first 45 minutes: like the player is shaping a real institution with tradeoffs.
- Over the full campaign: like the building has become socially consequential.
- In sandbox: like each new day can still generate obligations, stories, and repairs.
